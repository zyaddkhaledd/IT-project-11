Philae Temple


"Temple of Isis"

Philae Temple lies on Agilkia Island in the Nile near Aswan, blending Pharaonic, Hellenistic, and Roman artistry in a UNESCO‑relocated monument spared from the rising waters.

.

The front view of Philae Temple features two towering pylons carved with detailed scenes of pharaohs and gods. A stone pathway leads through graceful columns and floral capitals, creating a grand entrance that overlooks the Nile’s calm waters. Philae Temple lies gracefully on Agilkia Island, surrounded by the calm, blue waters of the Nile, creating one of the most picturesque settings in Egypt. Reflected in the river and framed by palm trees and rocky cliffs, the temple seems to float between water and sky. The peaceful atmosphere and natural beauty around it make the visit not only a journey through history but also a serene and unforgettable experience.


Philae Temple is located near Aswan and can only be reached by boat, offering a scenic ride across the Nile to Agilkia Island
It’s open daily, typically from 7:00 AM to 5:00 PM, with slightly extended hours in winter.

The columns of Philae Temple are beautifully decorated with floral capitals shaped like lotus, papyrus, and palm. Each one is uniquely carved with scenes of gods, symbols, and intricate patterns, reflecting both Egyptian and Greco-Roman influence. They add elegance and depth to the temple’s courtyards and halls. The columns of Philae Temple were mainly built during the Ptolemaic period (circa 3rd to 1st century BC), with later additions in the Roman period. These columns reflect a mix of traditional Egyptian and Greco-Roman artistic styles. Most are located in the temple’s courtyards and hypostyle halls, especially in the Temple of Isis.
